O Boticário's Mother's Day Experience: Designing a New Gifting Journey to Connect With Customers

Empowering customers to discover, compare, and choose the right Mother's Day gifts with confidence.

Role

UX Researcher
UX Designer

Year

2016

Duration

2 weeks

Sector

Beauty Retail

Project Overview

Boticário Group is one of Brazil's leading beauty companies, behind well-known brands like O Boticário, Quem Disse, Berenice?, Eudora, and Vult.

The aim of the project was to redesign the gift discovery journey of Mother's Day within a two-week sprint by strategically rethinking the user experience, identifying core usability issues, uncovering behavioral insights, and defining a more intuitive and resonant path to purchase to increase conversions.

The Problem

How might we transform a generic product listing into a guided gifting experience that helps customers choose with confidence?

Key Challenges

01
Generic, product-first experience
The Mother's Day page displayed a generic product grid with little visual hierarchy, focusing on products instead of helping customers find the right gift.
02
Limited gift discovery and filtering
Users had no dedicated gift search, meaningful filters, or guided recommendations to narrow down products based on recipient, preferences, or budget.
03
Missing emotional context
The experience lacked storytelling and emotional cues, failing to reflect the significance of Mother's Day and inspire confident gift decisions.

The Process

Working with the Lean UX Framework, I focused on delivering quick, validating iterations, balancing user needs, business goals, and technical constraints within a limited timeframe.

  1. Research and data analysis – Analyzed user behavior using heatmaps (Crazy Egg) and Google Analytics to identify drop-offs and friction points across key journeys.
  2. Stakeholder alignments – Led collaborative sessions with cross-functional teams to clarify goals, technical limitations, and success metrics.
  3. User personas and empathy mapping – Quickly defined key user types and mapped their motivations, frustrations, and emotional triggers to inform the experience design.
  4. Information architecture and wireframing – Designed and tested medium-fidelity wireframes and flow prototypes to validate ideas early with internal stakeholders.
  5. Hand-off and delivery – Delivered a fully reimagined experience and high-impact wireframes within the two-week sprint, setting the foundation for UI design and development.

Research and Data Analysis

To inform the redesign, quantitative and qualitative methods were combined to understand user behavior and business needs. This included analyzing heatmaps through Crazy Egg, reviewing Google Analytics to uncover user flows and drop-off points, and conducting stakeholder workshops to align strategic goals with customer expectations.

Insights & Opportunities

Through heatmap analysis and Google Analytics data, recurring patterns emerged across user behavior — revealing both the surface-level symptoms and the deeper friction points behind low campaign performance.

  • Price is the primary decision driver

    The heat concentration around the price range filter reinforces that price is a key decision factor for most users. It was their first move upon landing on the page. Opportunity: Prioritize price-range filtering by repositioning it as a primary, immediately visible interaction point on the page.

  • Users filter, not browse

    High interaction on the left sidebar filters combined with low engagement on the product grid suggests that users prefer filtering over browsing — indicating weak product presentation for a promotional campaign. Opportunity: Redesign the product layout and introduce guided filter flows that match users' mental model of narrowing down by preference rather than passive browsing.

  • No guided path for gift discovery

    Users lacked other ways to find a product beyond generic filters, revealing a need for a more personalized and intuitive discovery experience — similar to in-store guidance. Opportunity: Introduce a guided gift finder that mirrors the in-store experience by asking users about the recipient, budget, and preferences to surface relevant recommendations.

  • The layout failed to retain users

    Many users left the page quickly, showing that the layout failed to engage or guide them effectively upon arrival. Opportunity: Redesign the page structure to immediately communicate relevance and guide users toward meaningful product discovery, reducing early exits.

Stakeholder Alignments

I facilitated collaborative sessions with key stakeholders across marketing, product managers, and brand teams.

Through these sessions, I also gathered valuable insights from internal teams about customer behavior and pain points during previous Mother's Day campaigns. Together, we defined a UX strategy rooted in empathy and simplicity from these alignments.

Guiding Questions — In-Store Model

  • Who is this gift for?
  • What's your budget?
  • What does she enjoy?

This approach became the foundation for a more intuitive, emotionally resonant gifting journey prioritizing relevance, ease, and connection.

Key User Personas

I defined the personas based on key audience insights for O Boticário's Mother's Day campaign to guide design decisions based on real behavior and business priorities. The profiles were built using a mix of analytics, stakeholder inputs, past campaign learnings, and observed shopping behaviors, offering a strategic lens to design a more intuitive, emotional, and conversion-driven journey.

Information Architecture and Wireframe

To bring clarity and emotional resonance to O Boticário's Mother's Day page, I restructured the information architecture and designed the gift page to mimic the guidance of an in-store associate, helping users easily navigate by budget, recipient, or other preferences.

The final version of the wireframe was delivered after conducting rapid usability testing sessions with internal stakeholders. Based on their feedback gathered during these sessions, I refined the design to align better with user expectations and business objectives.

Designing the Experience

Price Filtering Optimization

I repositioned the previously overlooked price-filtering feature from the bottom to a prominent location at the top of the page, directly above a visually appealing image that captures the emotional essence of Mother's Day. This adjustment provided immediate visibility and easy access to the most frequently used filter, enhancing usability, reducing friction, and creating a more intuitive, budget-oriented shopping experience.

Persona-Based Navigation

Instead of guiding users through traditional product categories, the layout offers curated entry points based on meaningful relationships and recipient types — "Mãe também é" (Mother is also…).

This solution responds to user behavior insights showing a preference for filtering by characteristics rather than browsing generic product lists, while reinforcing brand values of care, diversity, and emotional storytelling.

  • Aunt – A maternal figure with a close emotional bond
  • Grandmother – A traditional and sentimental gift recipient
  • Godmother – Often youthful, stylish, and emotionally significant
  • Dad, who is also a mom – Inclusive representation of diverse families
  • Mother-in-law – Honored for her role in the extended family
  • Expecting mother – Celebrating new beginnings and motherhood in progress

Personalized Gift Discovery

To replicate more of the feeling of an in-store consultation, we designed a section that mimics how a salesperson might guide a shopper by asking, "What does she like?"

This conversational flow replaces the generic product grid with a more personalized and guided experience, making it easier for users to find thoughtful and relevant gifts.

  • The Fashionista – always staying on top of trends
  • The Home Decor Lover – finds joy in decorating with flowers
  • The Health-Conscious One – values self-care and takes time for herself
  • The Remarkable One – loves making a bold impression
  • The Explorer – enjoys traveling and relaxing

Handoff and Development

In 2016, handoff and collaboration between design and development relied on clear documentation, annotated wireframes, and close communication.

Once the redesigned wireframes were finalized and validated internally, I prepared detailed design specifications. Design files were shared with the development team via Dropbox, email, and intranet, supported by PDF documentation with usage notes and functional guidance.

To ensure alignment and consistency, I facilitated walkthrough sessions with front-end developers, addressing implementation questions and reviewing interactive behaviors.

Though modern tools like Figma or Zeplin were not yet standard, this process ensured a smooth and faithful transition from design to development within tight timelines. Close collaboration and weekly check-ins helped track progress and maintain design integrity throughout implementation.

Key Learnings

  • Emotional design drives conversions.

    Creating an experience that resonated with the emotional context of Mother's Day gift-giving directly impacted business results and how users interacted with the system.

  • Data-informed design decisions.

    Using analytics and heatmaps to understand user behavior prevented assumptions and led to more effective and creative solutions.

  • Guided discovery over overwhelming choice.

    Curating products into meaningful collections with clear navigation paths proved more effective than offering unlimited options at once.
